A chatbot prototype for pre-service teacher education grounded in CRP scholarship and the AERO Cultural Safety Framework

Hanham et al. · University of Macquarie

⚠ Important disclaimer: These chatbot conversations present exemplars reflecting specific research-derived cross-cultural primary school classroom scenarios. They do not represent the universal experiences of all members of the cultural group/s represented. Individual experiences can, and often will, vary within cultural groups. Students from CALD backgrounds often navigate syncretic identities, blending multiple cultural influences in ways not captured here. Undue extrapolation risks overlooking cultural nuances and complexities.

About this demo

Practise, reflect, and develop cultural judgement

Pre-service teachers often understand Culturally Responsive Pedagogy (CRP) in theory but lack opportunities to practise cultural judgement in realistic classroom contexts.

These two conversations demonstrate how the chatbot prototype simulates cross-cultural classroom scenarios, withholds the cultural explanation during the role-play (requiring the user to infer meaning), and provides structured feedback grounded in the AERO Cultural Safety Framework.

CALD Vignette

Aisha — "The Silent Yes"

Year 2 · Maths · Liverpool Public School · NSW

Aisha, 7, is Syrian-Australian. When her teacher asks who knows how many sides a triangle has, she responds with a quiet "Um… yes…" and then falls completely silent.

Cultural cue In Arabic-speaking household contexts, "yes" often signals active listening, not a full answer. An explicit invitation to speak is required before the student feels permitted to contribute.

First Nations Vignette

Jarrah — Peer Help-Seeking

Year 4 · Maths · Mascot Public School · NSW

Jarrah, 9, is Dharug Aboriginal Australian. During independent fraction work, he is observed three times in ten minutes leaning over to whisper to classmates and show his worksheet.

Cultural cue For many Aboriginal children, learning traditionally occurs through observation, collaboration, and peer-to-peer teaching within the kinship group. Seeking peer help is a culturally grounded strategy, not defiance.

Theoretical grounding

Three design decisions that make this culturally safe

01 · Context engineering

The chatbot draws only from a curated library of CRP scholarship — not LLM training defaults. Vignettes were developed through critical review of research on help-seeking in collectivist learning contexts.

02 · System prompt design

The cultural basis of each student's behaviour is withheld during role-play, requiring PSTs to infer meaning — developing conditional, not just declarative knowledge.

03 · Interactional guardrails

When a teacher response risks public shaming or deficit framing, the chatbot pauses role-play and prompts reflection — without revealing the underlying cultural dynamic.
